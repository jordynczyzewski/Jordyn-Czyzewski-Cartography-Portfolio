Stitching the Story

The Weight of Textiles

Jordyn Czyzewski GEOG 572

October 10, 2025

The global textile and apparel industry represents one of the most resource-intensive and spatially expansive sectors of the modern economy. Valued at over $1.7 trillion globally, it employs an estimated 430 million people across production, processing, and retail.

Despite this economic scale, the global textile system operates largely as a linear economy, where products are manufactured, sold, and quickly discarded.

Rapid globalization and the rise of fast fashion have dramatically accelerated textile output, pushing annual global production beyond 100 billion garments and intensifying the flow of materials through international trade networks.

Patterns of textile consumption and textile production link climate change, labor exploitation, and resource depletion within a single global system of exchange. Transitioning toward a circular textile system that prioritizes material recovery, reuse, and long-term value retention is beneficial yet requires coordinated economic and policy mechanisms that distribute responsibility across the entire supply chain, from manufacturing regions to consumer markets.

The United States boasts the highest per-capita consumption of new textiles and apparel globally, with a market value of $251.8 billion. 

While second only to China in absolute market share, this excess consumption makes the U.S. a leading contributor to textile global waste generation and one of the largest sources of secondary raw materials for circular supply chains, if collected, sorted, and pre-processed.

The EPA estimates that 85% of textiles in the U.S. are disposed of in landfills and incinerators and only 15% are diverted away from disposal.

Over 11.3 megatons of textile waste ends up in U.S. landfills every year since 2017, that is 80% more waste than in 2000.

Putting a Pin in California

Los Angeles is the production capital of U.S. apparel manufacturing, responsible for 83 percent of domestic cut-and-sew activity and housing 58 percent of all registered contractors. The LA Fashion District alone houses over 4,000 businesses involved in design, production, and wholesale. Just south in close proximity are the Ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach, where nearly 40 percent of all U.S. imports arrive.

 The Los Angeles market offers irreplaceable advantages for domestic brands and designers. Yet, wage theft remains rampant despite legislative intervention.

Decades of legislative intervention and government oversight have attempted to crack down on wage theft in the industry, which has one of the highest violation rates among low wage industries. The demographics of the workforce (primarily women, recent immigrants, and non-native English speakers) exacerbate the problem as they belong to marginalized communities. State and federal investigations regularly find millions of dollars a year in back wages owed, and studies of the local industry find an average wage that is a fraction of the state mandated minimum wage.

Taking Responsibility

In September 2024, California became the first U.S. state to enact a comprehensive Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) law for textiles. This marked a historic policy shift from voluntary recycling to mandatory producer accountability for apparel and household fabrics.

Under the Responsible Textile Recovery Act (SB 707), producers of clothing and textile products must:

  • Join or form a Producer Responsibility Organization (PRO), approved by the Department of Resources Recycling and Recovery (CalRecycle), by July 1, 2026.
  • Submit a statewide plan for the collection, repair, reuse, and recycling of post-consumer textiles for departmental review and public comment.
  • Implement the approved program no later than July 1, 2030, offering free collection and recovery for consumers across California.
  • Non-compliant companies face penalties of up to $50,000 per day for willful violations.

By requiring brands and producers to take responsibility for the entire life cycle of their products, the law indirectly pressures companies to tighten oversight of their supply chains. When a producer must finance the collection, reuse, and recycling of garments, the incentive shifts toward making fewer, higher-quality, and longer-lasting products, rather than fast-turnover disposable fashion. This shift can, over time, improve working conditions with enhanced standards.

New York anchors the other end of Americas textile industry

While Los Angeles anchors U.S. apparel production, New York City illustrates the next phase in the textile life cycle: consumption, collection, and reuse. Here, fashion and waste occupy the same landscape: luxury showrooms in Midtown, drop-off bins on street corners, and warehouses full of discarded fabrics.

The historic Garment District in Midtown Manhattan remains symbolic of domestic fashion and production. Meanwhile, the Port of New York and New Jersey connects East Coast trade to Europe and beyond.

New Yorkers purchase more apparel per capita than residents of any other U.S. city, yet most of those textiles end their lives in household trash. As supported by the NYC Department of Sanitation, clothing and household fabrics can also be brought to thrift stores, retail take-back programs, textile recyclers, or official drop-off bins. These items may be resold, donated, or reprocessed for industrial use as wiping rags, stuffing, or insulation.

New York's Public-Private Collection Systems

To capture these materials, the  NYC Department of Sanitation  and Housing Works created refashionNYC, a free in-building collection program for apartments, schools, and nonprofits. Over 1,300 bins citywide now funnel textiles into Housing Works reuse and recycling streams.

For residents without building bins, DSNY community drop-off sites operate ThursdaySaturday in all five boroughs, and Wearable Collections runs additional bins at major Greenmarkets: Union Square, Grand Army Plaza, Fort Greene, and others through a partnership with GrowNYC.

Collected items are sorted and reused through secondhand retailers or resold for reprocessing to make cleaning rags, seat padding, insulation, and more. DSNY acknowledges that post-collection routes vary. Some goods are reused domestically, others exported as secondhand clothing, and others shredded for industrial fiber. This opacity highlights a key challenge of circular systems: diversion does not guarantee recycling.

New York is now considering legislation that would extend its city-level circular systems to the entire state. Introduced in 2023 as Senate Bill S6654 / S3217, the proposal would establish a Textile Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) program under the oversight of the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC).

The proposed framework would require textile producers and retailers to manage the full life cycle of their products, including collection, reuse, recycling, and proper disposal.

This map demonstrates textile recycling facilities in the United States. New York has 15 of these textile recycling facilities.

By December 31, 2026, obligated entities would need to submit detailed collection and treatment plans to the DEC for public review and approval. Implementation would begin by July 1, 2027, and must provide free statewide textile drop-off services for consumers. Producers would also fund the costs of program administration, public outreach, and annual reporting to ensure transparency.

If enacted, New Yorks EPR law would mirror Californias SB 707 (Responsible Textile Recovery Act of 2024), creating the first eastwest policy alignment on textile responsibility in the United States. Where Californias law emerged from a production-side context aimed at regulating brands and factories within the nations garment capital, New Yorks proposal grows from a consumption-side reality, designed to manage waste streams in one of the worlds largest urban markets.

Together, these two initiatives reveal a broader policy convergence, recognizing that textile sustainability requires shared accountability across the supply chain.

Where do we go from here?

The U.S. textile economy extends beyond import and export trades. Every shipment represents expenditures in water, energy, and human labor. As environmental pressures grow, understanding where textiles come from and where they end up becomes critical in promoting sustainability. Cities like are experimenting with circular solutions: recycling programs, secondhand retail, and local manufacturing initiatives that can reduce waste and dependence on long-distance supply chains.
